# EIP-1559 Fee Market

## Part 1: How UltraFast uses EIP-1559 and why

UltraFast's working assumption for the EVM lane (§9.5) is **EIP-1559 base-fee dynamics on top of a governance-set floor**, with the scaling rule itself listed as an open decision in §16. The floor exists to throttle adversarial workloads from saturating execution budget; ordinary user programs pay gas at the prevailing fee market on top of the floor (§13.1). EIP-1559 is then the candidate rule for how that "above the floor" portion moves.

The motivation is conservative: EIP-1559 is the industry-standard fee mechanism on every major EVM chain, including Ethereum mainnet, Polygon PoS, BNB Chain, Avalanche C-Chain, every OP Stack chain, and Arbitrum (in modified form). Adopting it for the EVM lane keeps UltraFast's developer experience aligned with the rest of the EVM ecosystem — wallets, indexers, gas estimators, and tooling all assume EIP-1559 semantics. Departing from that default without strong reason would impose an integration tax on the EVM lane for no clear benefit.

**UltraFast deviations from the canonical EIP-1559 design.** Three differences are material:

1. **Governance-set floor.** Canonical EIP-1559 has no hard floor — the base fee can decay arbitrarily low when blocks are empty (in practice it is bounded by the 1 wei minimum). UltraFast's working assumption is that the base fee can fall to, but not below, a floor parameter set by on-chain UFAST-weighted vote (§13.1, §13). The floor acts as an anti-spam minimum, sized to make sustained block-saturation by junk transactions economically unattractive.
2. **Routing, not burning.** Canonical EIP-1559 burns the base fee. UltraFast denominates fees in BTC and routes the full stream — base fee plus priority tip — to stakers via the trading-fee distribution module (§13.1). There is no burn. The economic interpretation is the same on the demand side (the base fee is the inclusion price), but on the supply side the protocol monetises blockspace into stakeable yield rather than retiring it.
3. **Workload scope.** EIP-1559 applies only to the EVM lane. The gasless turing-incomplete order lane (§6.5–§6.6) does not use it. Orders bypass per-transaction gas accounting entirely and pay only the governance-set trading-fee schedule, deducted from account collateral at fill time. Pricing an order at the EVM-lane gas floor would couple market-maker quote-refresh cost to unrelated EVM demand and produce a fee surface institutional makers will not accept.

**Open in §16.** Three alternatives remain on the table:

- EIP-1559 base-fee dynamics on top of a governance-set floor (working assumption).
- A flat governance-set price, recalibrated by vote — simpler, but unresponsive to short-term demand.
- A per-block-fullness dynamic rule with no governance floor — closer to the original EIP-1559 spec, but without the anti-spam minimum.

UltraFast is pre-implementation; the choice will be fixed before genesis based on simulation work against expected EVM-lane workload.

## Part 2: EIP-1559 in detail

### Mechanism

EIP-1559 replaces the first-price gas auction that Ethereum used from genesis with a two-component fee:

- A **base fee per gas** set by protocol, paid by the sender and burned.
- A **priority fee per gas** (the "tip"), paid by the sender to the block proposer.

The base fee is recomputed deterministically every block from the prior block's gas usage relative to a **target** equal to half the block gas limit:

```
base_fee_next = base_fee * (1 + (1/8) * (gas_used - target) / target)
```

Two consequences fall out of the algebra:

- If a block is exactly at target, the next base fee is unchanged.
- If a block is full (`gas_used = 2 * target`), the next base fee rises by exactly 12.5 % (1/8). If a block is empty, it falls by exactly 12.5 %. The 1/8 constant is the maximum per-block change in either direction.

Blocks may exceed the target (up to 2× the target, which becomes the new max gas limit), but doing so increases the next block's base fee. The economic claim is that during sustained demand the base fee rises geometrically until demand and supply balance, at which point the fee stabilises near the marginal user's willingness to pay. The priority tip is then a small residual that prioritises among transactions willing to pay the base fee.

### Specification and deployment

EIP-1559 was authored by Vitalik Buterin, Eric Conti, Dan Finlay, Matthew Slipper, Ian Norden, and Abdelhamid Bakhta, with significant input from Tim Beiko's coordination work. It was deployed at the **London hardfork**, block 12,965,000, on **5 August 2021** on Ethereum mainnet.

The proposal had two stated goals: (1) reduce the variance and over-bidding inherent in first-price auctions by making inclusion pricing predictable, and (2) introduce a credible disinflation mechanism via base-fee burn. Both went live at London.

### Production status

EIP-1559 is now the default fee market on most production EVM chains:

- **Ethereum mainnet** (London, Aug 2021).
- **Polygon PoS** (Jan 2022).
- **BNB Chain** (since the Bruno upgrade, with parameter changes).
- **Avalanche C-Chain** (Apricot Phase 4, July 2021).
- **Every OP Stack chain** (Optimism, Base, etc.), with modified base-fee parameters tuned for L2 throughput.
- **Arbitrum**, with a modified version that combines EIP-1559-style base fee with a surge-pricing mechanism for L1 calldata costs.

### Extensions and refinements

The base mechanism has been extended several times:

- **EIP-4844 (proto-danksharding, Dencun, March 2024)** introduced a second EIP-1559-style market for **blob gas**, with its own base fee, target (3 blobs/block at launch), and adjustment rule. Blobs are the data-availability lane for rollups.
- **EIP-7691 (Pectra, 7 May 2025)** doubled the blob parameters: target from 3 to 6 blobs per block, maximum from 6 to 9. It also softened the adjustment asymmetry — the blob base fee now rises ~8.2 % per block when full and falls ~14.5 % per block when scarce, intended to keep blob pricing more stable as L2 demand grows.
- **EIP-7762** raised `MIN_BASE_FEE_PER_BLOB_GAS` to accelerate price discovery on blob space — at the original minimum, blob prices spent long stretches pinned to the floor before responding to demand.
- **EIP-7918 (proposed, post-Pectra)** bounds the blob base fee below by execution cost via a new constant `BLOB_BASE_COST`, preventing the blob fee signal from breaking when execution gas dominates the transaction.
- **EIP-7783 (informational)** proposes a controlled per-block gas-limit increase (e.g. +6 gas per block until a cap), so that the EIP-1559 target moves predictably rather than via ad hoc validator-set negotiation.
- **EIP-7999 (Buterin and Anders Elowsson)** generalises to a **unified multidimensional fee market** in which each transaction declares a single `max_fee` covering all resource dimensions (execution gas, blob gas, storage, etc.), with the protocol arbitraging that one cap across dimensions at inclusion time.
- **EIP-8011** specifies the underlying multidimensional gas-metering machinery EIP-7999 builds on.

### Economic impact

From August 2021 to early 2026, approximately **4.6 million ETH** has been burned via the EIP-1559 base-fee mechanism (per ultrasound.money tracking). However, burn rates collapsed sharply after Dencun pushed activity onto blob-backed L2s: daily mainnet burn fell from thousands of ETH at peak to roughly 50–70 ETH/day through 2025, against staker issuance of ~1,700 ETH/day. Net Ethereum issuance has been mildly positive (~0.23 % annualised) for most of 2025, which is the substantive content of the "ultrasound money narrative got complicated" discourse.

For UltraFast the burn-versus-route question is orthogonal: even if the burn signal weakens on Ethereum, the **pricing** mechanism of EIP-1559 — predictable base fee plus tip — is independent of whether the base fee is destroyed or redistributed.

### Comparison to alternative fee markets

- **First-price auction (pre-London Ethereum).** Users bid against each other; the highest bidders are included. Produces high variance in realised fees and systematic over-bidding because users do not know the marginal clearing price.
- **Solana's local fee markets (SIMD-0110, 2023 onward).** Solana keeps a low base fee but exposes account-level priority fees that respond to **per-account contention** — a transaction touching a hot writable account pays more, while a transaction touching cold accounts pays nothing extra. This addresses a known EIP-1559 limitation (see below) but at the cost of significantly more complex fee estimation. Users must reason about both global congestion and local hotspots on every contended writable account.
- **Aptos discrete-time auctions.** Aptos bundles transactions into rounds and runs an auction within each round, rather than continuous EIP-1559 adjustment.
- **MEV-Boost and the builder market.** EIP-1559 deliberately does not price MEV. The priority tip is the user's bid for inclusion; the proposer's MEV revenue (sandwich, back-running, top-of-block) is captured separately via builder auctions in the MEV-Boost stack. The "MEV-in-1559" debate concerns whether priority fees and MEV revenue should be unified or kept distinct; current consensus on Ethereum is to keep them distinct because MEV is structurally non-uniform across transactions while base-fee dynamics need to be uniform.

### Known limitations

1. **No locality.** EIP-1559's base fee is a **global** signal computed from total block utilisation. It does not respond to hotspots on individual storage slots, contracts, or accounts. A single highly contended contract can clear at very high effective tips while the base fee stays low because aggregate block utilisation is unchanged. Solana's local fee markets exist to address this; Ethereum's path is multi-dimensional gas (EIP-7999 / EIP-8011).
2. **Target-driven.** The mechanism targets 50 % utilisation by construction. It is provably **not** an efficient response to short-run demand spikes — the 12.5 %/block adjustment rate means that a true 10× demand shock takes roughly 20 blocks (~4 minutes on Ethereum mainnet) for the base fee to respond, during which time the priority-tip market does the work of an ordinary first-price auction.
3. **Floor pinning.** When demand is well below target, the base fee bottoms out (in Ethereum's case near 1 wei). Solana faces an analogous issue at the SOL minimum. In UltraFast's design the governance-set floor is a deliberate feature, not a bug: the goal is to keep the EVM lane defensible against junk traffic, not to extract maximum surplus during quiet periods.
4. **Block-target governance.** Increasing or decreasing the gas target (which sets the per-block work budget) requires social coordination — historically a contested process. EIP-7783 proposes a deterministic gas-target schedule to remove the discretion.

### Open protocol-level issues

The active research questions on Ethereum that translate directly to UltraFast's §16 decision are:

- **Dynamic target adjustment.** Should the target itself respond to sustained over- or under-utilisation, rather than being fixed by hardfork?
- **Multi-dimensional gas.** Should the fee market price execution, storage, and bandwidth separately (EIP-7999 / EIP-8011), or keep the single-dimensional model EIP-1559 was designed for?
- **Locality versus simplicity.** Does the chain accept Solana-style per-account fee markets to handle hotspots, or rely on a global signal and accept the locality limitation?
- **Interaction with proposer-builder separation.** With MEV-Boost-style builder auctions, priority tips become a smaller fraction of proposer revenue. Does that change the optimal split between base fee and tip?

For UltraFast the working assumption — vanilla EIP-1559 dynamics above a governance-set floor, with the full fee stream routed to BTC-denominated staker yield rather than burned — is conservative on the pricing side and divergent on the distribution side. The §16 decision will fix whether the dynamics piece is kept, dropped for a flat governance price, or replaced by a floor-less per-block-fullness rule. The distribution side (BTC-denominated, routed to stakers) is settled in §13.1 and does not depend on the §16 outcome.
